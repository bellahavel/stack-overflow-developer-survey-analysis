# Milestone 3 Narrative Draft

## Research Question

This project analyzes the 2025 Stack Overflow Developer Survey to explore the question: **What factors appear to influence salary and job satisfaction among professional developers?** The intended audience is not assumed to be deeply technical, so the goal is to present patterns clearly and responsibly rather than focus on complex statistical detail. The analysis focuses on respondents who identified themselves as professional developers, which keeps the sample aligned with the research question and avoids mixing hobbyists or learners into the same comparisons. The dataset was cleaned using a structured pipeline that converts salary and satisfaction fields to numeric values, removes duplicate responses, and documents missing data. A capped salary field is used in visualizations to prevent extreme outliers from distorting patterns. The analysis relies on grouped comparisons and summary statistics, which are presented through both static exploratory analysis and interactive dashboard views.

## Draft Findings

The strongest pattern in the analysis is that professional experience appears to be one of the most important drivers of salary. Median salary increases significantly across experience levels, especially when comparing early-career developers to mid-career and senior professionals. This trend is clearly visible in the dashboard’s salary-by-experience visualizations and remains consistent across multiple filtered views. Experience also shows a mild positive relationship with job satisfaction, suggesting that more experienced developers may benefit from increased stability, autonomy, or better role alignment over time.

A second major finding is that work arrangement plays an important role. Developers working in remote or flexible environments generally report higher salaries than those working fully in person. These groups also show slightly higher job satisfaction on average, although the difference in satisfaction is smaller than the difference in salary. This indicates that flexibility contributes to overall experience but is not the only factor shaping job satisfaction.

A third key result is the strong influence of geography. Because the Stack Overflow survey is global, salary levels vary significantly across countries, and global averages can therefore be misleading. To address this, the dashboard includes country filters and country-level comparison views that allow users to explore salary patterns within a more appropriate context. This adjustment highlights that some observed differences in salary may be driven more by location than by factors such as education or work arrangement.

The analysis also shows that salary and job satisfaction are only weakly related. While higher salaries are associated with slightly higher satisfaction, the relationship is not strong enough to treat salary as a complete measure of well-being. This suggests that non-monetary factors such as flexibility, work environment, and role fit play a meaningful role in shaping how developers feel about their jobs. In addition, the data indicates that modern developers typically work with multiple programming languages rather than a single technology stack. Languages such as JavaScript, SQL, Python, HTML/CSS, and TypeScript frequently appear together in responses, which supports the broader narrative that developer experience is shaped by combinations of tools, work structures, and career stages rather than isolated technical choices.

## Limitations and Next Steps

This analysis is observational and exploratory, meaning it identifies associations rather than causal relationships. Several variables may overlap in ways that complicate interpretation. For example, differences in salary across work arrangements may reflect differences in geography, company type, or experience level. Similarly, education and experience may interact with regional labor markets. In addition, some fields in the dataset, particularly salary and job satisfaction, contain missing values, so each visualization reflects only the subset of respondents who provided that information. This introduces potential bias that must be considered when interpreting results.

The interactive dashboard helps address some of these limitations by allowing users to filter by country, experience level, and work arrangement, which makes comparisons more transparent and reduces the risk of drawing conclusions from overly broad summaries. For the final project, the next steps include refining the dashboard design, improving the narrative flow within the interface, and narrowing the analysis to more controlled comparisons that better isolate key factors influencing salary and job satisfaction. At this stage, the central conclusion of the project is that salary is primarily driven by structural factors such as experience and geography, while job satisfaction depends on a broader combination of compensation, flexibility, and work context. Overall, developer outcomes are shaped less by any single variable and more by the interaction of multiple factors, which the dashboard is designed to explore in an intuitive and interactive way.
