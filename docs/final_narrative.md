# Final Narrative

## Research Question

This report analyzes the 2025 Stack Overflow Developer Survey to address the question: What factors influence salary and job satisfaction among professional developers? The analysis is intended for a non-technical audience, highlighting clear patterns rather than detailed statistical methods. Only professional developers are included in the analysis, keeping the sample aligned with the research question and avoiding the inclusion of hobbyists or learners. The dataset underwent a cleaning procedure that converted salary and satisfaction fields to numeric values, removed duplicate responses, and documented missing data. A capped salary field was introduced to prevent extreme outliers from distorting visualizations. Findings are presented through grouped comparisons and summary statistics, initially explored in a static exploratory data analysis (EDA) notebook and now through an interactive dashboard.

## Findings

An important finding from the analysis challenges a common assumption: salary and job satisfaction are only weakly correlated. It is often expected that higher-paying positions lead to greater satisfaction. The data shows that although a positive association exists, the relationship is minimal. Salary alone does not dependably predict job satisfaction. This insight reframes the analysis, indicating that compensation is not the sole determinant of satisfaction. Instead, developers appear to consider flexibility, work environment, and role fit in addition to salary when evaluating and reporting their job satisfaction.

Remote work data further supports this conclusion. Developers who work remotely or in flexible arrangements earn significantly more than those working fully on-site, yet their job satisfaction scores are only slightly higher. The salary gap is substantial and consistent, whereas the satisfaction gap remains modest. While flexibility appears to contribute to satisfaction, the modest satisfaction gap suggests that it does not shape developers' overall job perceptions as strongly as salary differences might lead us to expect. These patterns suggest that focusing solely on salary or remote access is unlikely to substantially improve job satisfaction.

Experience is a more dependable predictor of salary. Median compensation increases consistently across career stages, with the largest rise observed between early- and mid-career developers. This trend is clear across multiple filtered views in the dashboard and represents the most consistent structural pattern in the dataset. Experience also demonstrates a mild positive relationship with satisfaction, likely reflecting increased autonomy, stability, and role alignment over time, though the effect remains modest.

Geography is another significant factor influencing the data and calls for careful consideration to avoid misinterpretation. Given the global scope of the Stack Overflow survey, country-level salary distributions vary substantially, making unfiltered averages possibly misleading. The dashboard handles this by providing country filters and country-level comparison views, supporting more accurate and fair comparisons. This approach also clarifies that some observed differences, such as those between education levels or work arrangements, may primarily reflect geographic variation.

## Limitations and Next Steps

This analysis relies on self-reported survey data, which means responses may contain reporting errors, rounding, subjective interpretation, or response bias. Additionally, the analysis is observational and exploratory; it identifies associations rather than causes. Many variables under consideration are interrelated, making it difficult to isolate their individual effects. For example, salary differences between remote and on-site workers may result from underlying disparities in geography, company size, or experience level rather than work arrangement alone. This overlap is genuine, and filtering can narrow it but not eliminate it entirely.

Another limitation is missing data. Salary and job satisfaction, the two primary variables in this analysis, show substantial rates of non-response. As a result, each visualization shows only the developers who answered these questions. It remains unknown whether non-respondents differ systematically from respondents, introducing potential bias that must be considered when interpreting the results.

The interactive dashboard is intended to make these limitations more transparent and manageable. Filters for country, experience level, and work arrangement enable users to improve comparisons, thus lowering the risk of drawing overly broad conclusions. The central finding of the project is that salary is mainly affected by factors such as experience and geography, whereas job satisfaction is determined by a broader set of factors, including compensation, flexibility, and working conditions. No single variable is dominant, which underscores the value of allowing users to explore these interactions rather than relying on a fixed conclusion.
